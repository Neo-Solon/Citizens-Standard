## Working Title and Positioning

**Working title:** *The Citizens Standard as Counterfactual Benchmark: Empirical Analysis of an Alternative US Monetary Architecture, 1965–2025*

**Positioning relative to the architectural paper:** This paper is a companion to *The Citizens Standard: A Constitutional Monetary Architecture with Mode-Selectable Inflation Regimes* (Neo-Solon, 2026), which proposed the framework's architecture and three Mode parameterizations. The present paper takes that architecture as given and asks an empirical question: applied to actual US economic data over the past sixty years, what outcomes would the framework have produced for representative citizens, and how would those outcomes compare to what citizens actually experienced under the discretionary monetary system?

The present paper makes no claims about the framework's normative desirability that the architectural paper does not already make. It also makes no predictions about future performance. It is a backward-looking counterfactual analysis grounded in observable historical data, intended to give critics and supporters alike a concrete empirical reference point for evaluating the architectural proposal.

## What This Paper Claims, and What It Does Not

### Claims

1. Under conservative parameter assumptions and using actual US historical data for M2, GDP, population, equity returns, and CPI from 1960 through 2025, the Citizens Standard's Mode B configuration would have produced a Stable Floor at age 65 substantially larger than the median actually-realized US retirement account balance for the same cohort.

2. This finding holds across four representative birth cohorts (1960, 1970, 1980, 1990) — though the magnitude of the gap varies by cohort, and the youngest two cohorts (1980, 1990) involve forward projection to retirement that we report with explicit confidence bands.

3. The finding is robust to plausible variation in equity-return assumptions: Mode B's Stable Floor exceeds the median actual outcome under historical-average returns (~6.5% real), forward-looking forecast returns (~4.5% real), and pessimistic scenarios (~3% real).

4. The framework's structural advantage over actual outcomes is concentrated in three architectural features: equal per-citizen distribution at issuance (eliminating individual savings discipline as a precondition for retirement security), automatic total-market index allocation (eliminating concentration risk and fee drag), and the constitutional lock through age 65 (eliminating early withdrawal as a leakage channel).

### Non-claims

1. **This paper does not predict future performance.** All projections beyond 2025 are explicitly labeled as projections under specified assumptions, and we report ranges rather than point estimates wherever the underlying data is not yet observed.

2. **This paper does not claim that observed outcomes would have been the same under the Citizens Standard.** A counterfactual world with the framework operating would have had different equity valuations, different savings behavior, and different Federal Reserve actions than the world we observed. We hold equity total returns and CPI exogenous to the framework as a methodological simplification, and we acknowledge this is a strong assumption (see Methodological Limitations below).

3. **This paper does not analyze Modes A or C empirically.** Mode A's deflation regime would have produced second-order effects on debt contracts and wage rigidity that cannot be modeled without behavioral assumptions we cannot ground in observed data. Mode C's K3 dividend would have altered citizen consumption and savings behavior in ways that are similarly unobservable historically. Mode B is the only Mode whose counterfactual can be run with reasonable methodological discipline, because its target — approximate price stability — is closest to what the actual Federal Reserve has claimed to target throughout the analysis period.

4. **This paper does not advocate for adoption of the Citizens Standard.** The architectural paper (Neo-Solon, 2026) makes the framework's normative case. The present paper is descriptive: here are the numbers under the architectural counterfactual; readers should weigh those numbers alongside the architectural paper's arguments and form their own conclusions.

## Cohort Selection and Rationale

We analyze four representative birth cohorts: citizens born in 1960, 1970, 1980, and 1990. Each cohort experiences a different mix of economic conditions across their working life, providing variation that prevents the analysis from being driven by any single historical period.

### Cohort A: Born 1960

Working life spans roughly 1982–2025 (age 22 to age 65). This cohort begins working immediately after the Volcker disinflation, builds savings through the long bull market of the 1980s and 1990s, experiences the dot-com crash mid-career, the Global Financial Crisis at age 48, and COVID-19 at age 60. They retire in 2025 at age 65 — a fully observable, retrospective cohort. All inputs to the counterfactual are realized historical data. This cohort serves as the analysis's empirical anchor.

### Cohort B: Born 1970

Working life 1992–2035 (projected retirement). This cohort enters the workforce during the dot-com boom, experiences both 2000-era and 2008-era equity crashes during their accumulation years, and is currently mid-career at the time of analysis. We have observed data through 2025 (their first 33 working years) and project forward to age 65 in 2035 under three equity-return assumptions: pessimistic, central, optimistic.

### Cohort C: Born 1980

Working life 2002–2045 (projected retirement). This cohort starts work during the dot-com aftermath, experiences the GFC at the start of their career rather than mid-career, and the COVID disruption at age 40. We have observed data through 2025 (their first 23 working years) and project forward.

### Cohort D: Born 1990

Working life 2012–2055 (projected retirement). This cohort enters the workforce during the post-GFC recovery and has so far experienced predominantly favorable equity conditions. We have observed data through 2025 (their first 13 working years) and project the remaining 30 years.

### Why these four

Together, these cohorts span sixty years of variation in economic conditions, with each exposed to a different mix of expansion and crisis at different career stages. The 1960 and 1970 cohorts experienced significant working-life crises mid-career (when accumulated balances were largest, and therefore most vulnerable to drawdown). The 1980 and 1990 cohorts experienced significant crises early in their careers (when accumulated balances were smaller and recovery time longer). This variation tests whether the framework's performance depends on cohort-specific luck.

The youngest cohort (1990) is included primarily as a sensitivity test: any framework can look good when applied retrospectively to a cohort whose retirement is forty years away. The interesting question is whether the framework's projected performance for that cohort, under conservative assumptions, still differs meaningfully from the projected actual outcome under conservative assumptions. We return to this in the sensitivity analysis section.

## Methodology

### Step 1: Reconstruct historical inputs

For each year from 1960 through 2025, we extract M2, nominal GDP, real GDP growth, population, and CPI from the sources above. These five variables are the inputs the Citizens Standard's K1 and K2 formulas require.

### Step 2: Apply Mode B's K1 and K2 calibration

Mode B's calibration (per the architectural paper, Section 5):
- **K1 = 2.5% of GDP per capita per new verified citizen at birth**
- **K2 = half of (real growth × M2) less K1 total, distributed equally to all living citizens**

For each cohort, we compute the K1 deposit at birth (a function of GDP per capita in their birth year) and the per-citizen K2 deposit each year (a function of M2 × real growth in each year of their life).

### Step 3: Apply equity returns to accumulate the Stable Floor

The Stable Floor accumulates K1 and K2 deposits at the realized historical S&P 500 real return (from the Shiller dataset) for each year of the cohort's life. We use the **real** total return series rather than nominal because the framework's outcomes are reported in launch-year purchasing power throughout.

### Step 4: Compute Stable Floor at age 65

For Cohort A (born 1960), age 65 is 2025 — fully retrospective. For Cohorts B–D, age 65 is in the future, and we report projected Stable Floor at retirement under three equity return scenarios for the remaining years.

### Step 5: Compute counterfactual income

We assume a 4% safe withdrawal rate per the standard convention (Bengen 1994). The framework's specified 5% withdrawal cap (architectural paper, Section 3.7) would produce roughly 25% higher annual income; we use the more conservative 4% number for the comparison so that any difference vs. actual outcomes is not driven by the withdrawal-rate assumption.

### Step 6: Compare to actual outcomes

For the comparison to actual outcomes, we use:
- **Median 401(k) + IRA balance at retirement age**: Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances, age 65–74 cohort, most recent year for which the cohort under analysis has data.
- **Average 401(k) + IRA balance at retirement age**: Same source.
- **Social Security average benefit**: SSA Monthly Statistical Snapshot, retirement-aged retired worker.
- **SS trust fund projection scenario**: For cohorts retiring after 2034, we report outcomes both with full SS benefits and with the 23% benefit cut projected by the 2025 SSA Trustees Report.

### Step 7: Sensitivity analysis

We re-run the counterfactual under three equity-return assumptions (pessimistic 3% real, central 4.5% real, historical-average 6.5% real), three K2 calibration variants (the paper-stated 50% of real growth, plus 25% and 75% sensitivity bounds), and two Mode B parameter alternatives (the paper's "approximate stability with mild deflationary drift" calibration vs. an exact-real-growth-match calibration that targets zero inflation more strictly).

### Step 8: Stress-test against historical adverse sequences

We also re-run the counterfactual using actual year-by-year equity returns and CPI for three historical adverse sequences (1929–1944 Great Depression, 1966–1982 stagflation, 2000–2009 lost decade), each applied as if it occurred during the cohort's prime accumulation years (ages 25–45). This is the same stress-testing methodology used in the interactive engine that accompanies the architectural paper.

## Methodological Limitations

This section names the analytical risks that any honest counterfactual analysis must address. Each is a real limitation, not a defensive hedge.

### Limitation 1: Survivorship bias in historical equity returns

The US equity market is the survivor of the twentieth century. Russia, China, Argentina, and Germany had functioning equity markets in 1900 that effectively went to zero through war, revolution, hyperinflation, or expropriation. The Shiller dataset, which we use throughout, reflects the experience of one survivor.

The Citizens Standard's success under Mode B depends on the equity transmission channel functioning over decades. If the United States experiences a non-survivor outcome at any point during a cohort's working life — sustained civil collapse, currency repudiation, sovereign default — Mode B's Stable Floor would be substantially eroded or destroyed. The framework's Market Exit provision (architectural paper, Section 10) provides constitutional recourse in such scenarios, but recourse is not the same as protection.

We address this limitation in two ways. First, we present the full range of stressed outcomes alongside the central estimate, including the worst observed historical sequences. Second, we include a brief comparative section showing what Mode B's Stable Floor would have produced if applied to several non-survivor economies (Argentina, Russia post-1917, Germany 1923, Japan post-1990) to give the reader a sense of how the framework performs when the assumed equity-return path fails. The honest answer in those cases is that the framework performs poorly — which is also true of every other equity-based retirement system.

### Limitation 2: Regime endogeneity

Real-world equity returns, CPI, and M2 expansion during a hypothetical Citizens Standard era would have differed from the realized values we observe under the actual Federal Reserve regime. Specifically: if the framework had been operating, the Fed would not have conducted the QE programs of 2008–2014 and 2020–2022, M2 expansion would have followed the framework's K1+K2 path rather than the discretionary path, and equity valuations would have been different. We cannot observe the counterfactual world.

The methodological choice we make is to hold equity returns and CPI exogenous to the framework — that is, we apply the framework's K1+K2 formulas to *actual* historical M2, GDP, and equity-return data. This produces a hybrid counterfactual: the framework's issuance rules apply, but the macroeconomic environment is what actually happened. We acknowledge this is a strong assumption.

The case for this choice is methodological discipline: any attempt to model the counterfactual macroeconomy introduces additional assumptions whose joint effect would be impossible to disentangle from the framework's effect. The hybrid counterfactual produces a defensible lower bound on what the framework would have produced, since any plausible counterfactual macroeconomy would likely have featured lower discretionary M2 expansion (and therefore stronger purchasing power for K2 deposits than actually realized).

### Limitation 3: Confirmation bias in scenario selection

It is possible to select historical periods where any framework looks good. We address this in three ways. First, we use multiple cohorts spanning sixty years, which eliminates single-period selection. Second, we apply stress sequences from the worst observed historical periods (Great Depression, 1970s stagflation, 2000s lost decade), which captures adversarial cases. Third, we pre-commit in this methodology section to reporting all results, including those where the framework underperforms or produces ambiguous outcomes. If the data shows Mode B does not improve on actual outcomes for some cohort or some sensitivity range, we report it.

### Limitation 4: Behavioral and structural advantages confounded

Some of the framework's apparent advantage over actual outcomes derives not from monetary architecture but from the framework's elimination of real-world behavioral and structural problems: undersaving, early withdrawal, fee drag, asset concentration, and lapses during periods of unemployment or financial stress. A median American who could and did save 10% of income from age 22, allocated to a low-cost total market index, never withdrew early, never panic-sold during downturns, and never paid above 0.05% in fees would also retire with much more than the median observed balance.

The framework does not invent the optimization. It eliminates the possibility of suboptimization. We discuss this distinction explicitly: the Citizens Standard's structural advantage over actual outcomes combines (a) monetary innovation in the form of seigniorage redirection to citizens, and (b) structural elimination of behavioral leakages that affect real-world retirement saving. The two are conceptually separable and we report both decompositions where the data permits.

### Limitation 5: Defined-benefit pensions and other retirement assets

The Survey of Consumer Finances measures balances in defined-contribution accounts (401(k), IRA) and similar individual retirement vehicles. It does not include the present value of defined-benefit pension claims, which were more common for the 1960 cohort than for younger cohorts. For cohorts with substantial DB pension coverage, the SCF understates total retirement wealth. We address this by reporting both raw SCF figures and adjusted figures incorporating Census Bureau estimates of DB pension wealth where available. For the 1960 cohort specifically, the adjustment is non-trivial and we report both numbers.

## Pre-Committed Reporting Standards

To preempt confirmation-bias concerns, we pre-commit to the following reporting standards before running the analysis:

1. **All four cohorts will be reported, including any whose results are unfavorable to the framework.**

2. **All three equity-return scenarios (3%, 4.5%, 6.5%) will be reported, not only the central case.**

3. **All four stress sequences (smooth baseline, Great Depression, stagflation, lost decade) will be reported.**

4. **Any cohort or scenario where Mode B's Stable Floor falls below the median actual outcome will be highlighted, not buried.**

5. **The decomposition of "framework advantage" between monetary architecture and elimination of behavioral leakages will be explicit.**

6. **Non-survivor economy comparisons (Argentina, Russia, Germany, Japan) will be reported in the limitations section, not omitted.**

7. **Code and data will be released with the paper at github.com/Neo-Solon/Citizens-Standard, allowing third-party replication.**
