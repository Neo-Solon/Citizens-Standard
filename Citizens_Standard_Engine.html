What you're looking at

The Citizens Standard is a constitutional monetary framework that replaces central-bank discretion with three rules-based issuance channels. Every dollar of new money is distributed equally to all citizens — split between locked citizen equity (the Stable Floor) and, in inflation Mode, monthly dividends.

How to use: Pick a Mode below to see one of the framework's three constitutional configurations. Adjust any slider to build your own. The stat cards and charts update live. The vs. real outcomes tab compares your configuration to what Americans actually retire with under today's system.

K1: % of GDP per capita per new citizen. K2 — Growth rate: the share of the real-growth-matched budget that is issued — 100% holds prices stable (Mode B), ~12.5% gives mild deflation (Mode A). This sets how much new money is created. K2/K3 split (κ_d): of that one budget, how much locks into Stable Floors (K2) versus pays out as a monthly dividend (K3) — the two shares sum to 100% and the split is price-neutral (it moves money between locked and spendable, not the total). KI — Inflation-gap: % of M2 issued above the growth line; the only channel that creates inflation (Mode C).

Reading the comparison: "Wealth at 65" is locked savings/equity. "Lifetime cash" is total dividends/transfers received during working life and retirement. "Total real value" sums both — this is the fairest single number for cross-system comparison since Modes B and C make different tradeoffs between locked wealth and ongoing dividends. Mode B is the cleanest apples-to-apples comparison with the Fed status quo since both target price stability, and under identical return assumptions it produces the largest locked Stable Floor (it issues at the full real-growth-matched rate). Mode A optimizes for rising real wages and purchasing-power preservation — every dollar a citizen holds gains value — at the cost of a smaller locked floor and no income stream; Mode C optimizes for a monthly dividend (gives up locked wealth). Citizens Standard delivers equity to every citizen automatically — no savings discipline required. Real-world systems require individual savings effort that most Americans never achieve.

Inflation paths: Each system's CPI trajectory over 65 years. The Citizens Standard rate is derived from your issuance settings, not assumed. The Citizens Standard's three Modes are deliberate constitutional targets. Other systems' inflation paths reflect their actual or theoretical behavior.

The Citizens Standard line (thick green) is computed from your channel settings (issuance minus real growth) — raise KI and watch it climb into inflation; set K2 to 100% with KI off and it sits at zero. Raising K3 (κ_d) pays a dividend without moving this line — it reallocates within the budget. Fed status quo shows historical reality with stylized shock periods (recession episode and inflation spike). Bitcoin's deflation tracks your real growth rate (fixed supply meets growing economy). What this answers: "How does the framework retire the national debt — and tighten when needed?" Under Mode T, citizen K1/K2 keep running at full Mode-B price stability, while KT retires the legacy debt down to a small operational floor — not to zero. The floor is kept on purpose: it's the financial system's safe-asset benchmark and the standing base for monetary operations. KT runs symmetrically: it retires excess debt in normal times, and in reverse — issuing short-term bills to drain transactional money — when inflation runs hot. Retiring debt as a fiscal burden (interest, rollover risk) is the goal; the small remaining stock is debt held by choice, as a tool.

The inflation-shock scenario shows reverse-KT issuing bills to soak up money — the stock lifts above the floor, then normalizes. ⚠ This is an illustrative projection, not a guarantee.

The headline numbers (e.g., $1.9M Stable Floor at retirement under Mode B defaults) assume steady 6.5% real equity returns over 65 years with no major crashes, recessions, or secular slowdowns. Real-world outcomes will almost certainly differ — possibly by hundreds of thousands of dollars in either direction. Forward-looking equity return forecasts from Vanguard, Research Affiliates, and AQR currently project 4-5% real for the next decade, not the long-run historical 6.5%. Use the Stress Test tab to see what the framework actually delivers under historical adverse sequences (Great Depression, 1970s stagflation, 2000s lost decade). The Citizens Standard's structural advantage over the status quo is robust, but the specific dollar figures depend heavily on assumptions you can adjust. Treat all projections as "could be very large wealth if conditions cooperate" rather than promises.

Real-world data sources

401(k) balances: Vanguard, How America Saves 2025. Median balance at age 55-64 is $95,642; average is $271,320. Median is more representative of typical outcomes; the average is skewed by high-balance accounts.

Social Security: SSA Monthly Statistical Snapshot, March 2026. Average retired-worker benefit: $2,079.49/month ($24,953/year). Replacement rate for the average earner: ~37-42% of late-career earnings (CBO, SSA 2024-2025).

Trust fund depletion: 2025 SSA Trustees Report projects the OASI trust fund will be unable to pay full benefits beginning around 2034, after which only ~77-81% of scheduled benefits would be payable.

Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend: Alaska Department of Revenue. Recent dividends ranged $1,200-$3,300/year; default in this engine is $1,700.

Bitcoin: Treated as a savings vehicle at the user-set equity return for fair comparison. Real Bitcoin returns have been wildly volatile and are not predictive.

Engine methodology

Single-cohort projection: tracks one citizen born at year 0. K1 deposited once at birth; K2 deposited annually into the locked floor; K3 (κ_d), when active, reallocates that share of the K2 budget into a monthly dividend; KI, when active, adds issuance above the growth line. Stable Floor compounds at the equity return rate. Real values deflate by CPI index. Each year, M2 expands by total issuance, GDP grows at real growth × CPI, and population grows at the pop-growth rate.

For comparison columns, "median outcome" uses real-world median 401(k) balance ($95,642) at retirement age and actual SS average benefit. "Average outcome" uses Vanguard's average 401(k) balance ($271,320) and average SS. "After SS trust depletion" applies a 23% cut to Social Security benefits per Trustees' projections. None of these scenarios assume perfect savings discipline over 43 years — they reflect what Americans actually retire with.

Honest caveats

Inflation is derived from issuance (total new money as a share of M2, minus real growth), a quantity-theory approximation of the paper's price-level mechanism rather than its full targeting formula. K3 (κ_d) reallocates a share of the growth budget into a price-neutral dividend — the engine treats it as fully absorbed by the transactional circuit, so it lowers the floor without moving prices; beyond the M^T ceiling it would instead add to inflation (Mode C). KI is modeled as a flat percent of M2 issued above the growth line, and is the channel that moves the price level. Equity return compounds the Stable Floor; real values are obtained by deflating with the derived price index, so deflationary Mode A shows a purchasing-power boost and inflationary Mode C is eroded. Births computed as population × pop-growth (real demographics are more complex). The full paper's projections include stochastic returns, mode transitions, sequence-of-returns risk, and emergency tools — this engine is for intuition, not policy calibration.
