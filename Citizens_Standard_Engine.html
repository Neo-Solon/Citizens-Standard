What you're looking at

The Citizens Standard is a constitutional monetary framework that replaces central-bank discretion with three rules-based issuance channels. Every dollar of new money is distributed equally to all citizens — split between locked citizen equity (the Stable Floor) and, in inflation Mode, monthly dividends.

How to use: Pick a Mode below to see one of the framework's four constitutional configurations. Adjust any slider to build your own. The stat cards and charts update live. The vs. real outcomes tab compares your configuration to what Americans actually retire with under today's system.

How this differs from other monetary proposals: a UBI pays a monthly cheque, but it is funded by taxes the same people pay — for a median earner it nets to roughly a wash. MMT’s jobs guarantee provides a paycheck for work, not wealth. Bitcoin, the Chicago Plan, and Friedman’s k-rule change who controls money creation but route none of it to citizens. The Citizens Standard is the only one that hands newly-created money to every citizen as locked, equal, rules-based wealth — on top of the same private savings (median 401(k) ~$95,642) and Social Security (~$24,953/yr) everyone already has.

Reading the modes: Mode B builds the largest locked floor and pays no dividend; Mode A is smaller because it issues less into the floor, not because deflation erodes it (deflation slightly helps); Modes C and D trade locked wealth for a spendable monthly dividend, so their floor is lower — dollars paid out as income don’t compound for decades the way the floor does. All figures are in constant launch-year dollars; they don’t capture each mode’s separate effect on the purchasing power of wages and cash (deflation helps, inflation taxes), so Mode A is worth a little more than its numbers and the inflationary modes a little less.

What this answers: "How does the framework retire the national debt — and tighten when needed?" Under Mode T, citizen K1/K2 keep running at full Mode-B price stability, while KT retires the legacy debt down to a small operational floor — not to zero. The floor is kept on purpose: it's the financial system's safe-asset benchmark and the standing base for monetary operations. KT runs symmetrically: it retires excess debt in normal times, and in reverse — issuing short-term bills to drain transactional money — when inflation runs hot. Retiring debt as a fiscal burden (interest, rollover risk) is the goal; the small remaining stock is debt held by choice, as a tool.

What this answers: "What if conditions change — aging, depopulation, an AI productivity boom?" Mode Ω is the adaptive configuration: instead of fixed channel settings, its K1, K2, and KI respond automatically to demographic and productivity conditions by published formula — no committee, no discretion. In calm conditions it runs conservatively (≈60% K2 capture, mild deflationary bias, no KI); its governors engage only when conditions warrant and revert at 25%/year once conditions normalize. Pick a scenario to watch the governors respond; the lifetime figures are Paper 1 §8.6's simulation results.

Mode Ω guardrails (Architecture §8): every multiplier, threshold, and reversion rate is formula-specified and publicly auditable — adaptive means the formula responds to observable data, not that anyone exercises judgment. K1 is capped at 2.0× base; combined issuance is capped at 3.5% of M2; the conditional KI issues 0–0.6% of M2 (split 60% to Stable Floors / 40% spendable), activates only on sustained deflation (>1.2%) or demographic stress with weak wage growth, and carries a 36-month sunset with mandatory reaffirmation. The headline numbers (e.g., the ~$0.74M Mode B Stable Floor at the 4.5% real central default, or ~$1.83M at the 6.5% historical preset) assume steady real equity returns over 65 years with no major crashes, recessions, or secular slowdowns. Real-world outcomes will almost certainly differ — possibly by hundreds of thousands of dollars in either direction. Forward-looking equity return forecasts from Vanguard, Research Affiliates, and AQR currently project 4-5% real for the next decade, not the long-run historical 6.5%. Use the Stress Test tab to see what the framework actually delivers under historical adverse sequences (Great Depression, 1970s stagflation, 2000s lost decade). The Citizens Standard's structural advantage over the status quo is robust, but the specific dollar figures depend heavily on assumptions you can adjust. Treat all projections as "could be very large wealth if conditions cooperate" rather than promises.

Real-world data sources

401(k) balances: Vanguard, How America Saves 2025. Median balance at age 55-64 is $95,642; average is $271,320. Median is more representative of typical outcomes; the average is skewed by high-balance accounts.

Social Security: SSA Monthly Statistical Snapshot, March 2026. Average retired-worker benefit: $2,079.49/month ($24,953/year). Replacement rate for the average earner: ~37-42% of late-career earnings (CBO, SSA 2024-2025).

Trust fund depletion: 2025 SSA Trustees Report projects the OASI trust fund will be unable to pay full benefits beginning around 2034, after which only ~77-81% of scheduled benefits would be payable.

Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend: Alaska Department of Revenue. Recent dividends ranged $1,200-$3,300/year; default in this engine is $1,700.

Bitcoin: Treated as a savings vehicle at the user-set equity return for fair comparison. Real Bitcoin returns have been wildly volatile and are not predictive.

Engine methodology

Single-cohort projection: tracks one citizen born at year 0. K1 deposited once at birth; K2 deposited annually into the locked floor; K3 (κ_d), when active, reallocates that share of the K2 budget into a monthly dividend; KI, when active, adds issuance above the growth line. The Stable Floor's real value compounds at the real equity return (uniform across Modes). Dividends and cash are shown in real, CPI-deflated terms. Each year, M2 expands by total issuance, GDP grows at real growth × CPI, and population grows at the pop-growth rate.

For comparison columns, "median outcome" uses real-world median 401(k) balance ($95,642) at retirement age and actual SS average benefit. "Average outcome" uses Vanguard's average 401(k) balance ($271,320) and average SS. "After SS trust depletion" applies a 23% cut to Social Security benefits per Trustees' projections. None of these scenarios assume perfect savings discipline over 43 years — they reflect what Americans actually retire with.

Honest caveats

Inflation is derived from issuance (total new money as a share of M2, minus real growth), a quantity-theory approximation of the paper's price-level mechanism rather than its full targeting formula. K3 (κ_d) reallocates a share of the growth budget into a price-neutral dividend — the engine treats it as fully absorbed by the transactional circuit, so it lowers the floor without moving prices; beyond the M^T ceiling it would instead add to inflation (Mode C). KI is modeled as a flat percent of M2 issued above the growth line, and is the channel that moves the price level. The Stable Floor holds total-market equity, so its real value compounds at the real equity return, uniform across Modes — a real asset's real return does not depend on the Mode's CPI regime. Deflation and inflation therefore act on the circulating pool (cash, wages, and the KI/K3 dividends, shown in real terms), not on the real value of the locked equity floor: Mode A's deflation appears as rising real wages and appreciating cash rather than a larger floor. Births computed as population × pop-growth (real demographics are more complex). The full paper's projections include stochastic returns, mode transitions, sequence-of-returns risk, and emergency tools — this engine is for intuition, not policy calibration.
